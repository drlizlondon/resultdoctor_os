STEP 4.1 URGENT EXPLICIT Admit — cardiovascular instability ▼
IF cardiovascular instability (e.g. postural drop)
THEN ADMIT urgently
Patient with iron deficiency (suspected or confirmed) who is haemodynamically unstable — postural hypotension or equivalent cardiovascular compromise — requires immediate hospital admission. This takes priority over all other pathway branches.
  • 🚨URGENT: Admit to hospital immediately
  • 📞Contact receiving team / call 999 as appropriate for clinical situation
"Admit if unstable cardiovascularly e.g. postural drop"

STEP 4.1 / 12.0 2WW EXPLICIT 2-Week Wait referral to gastroenterology ▼
IF (age ≥40 AND jaundice AND (unexplained weight loss OR abdominal pain))
OR (age <50 AND rectal bleeding AND (abdominal pain OR change in bowel habit OR weight loss OR iron deficiency anaemia))
OR (age ≥50 AND unexplained rectal bleeding)
OR (age ≥60 AND (iron deficiency anaemia OR bowel habit change OR weight loss) AND any of: diarrhoea/back pain/abdominal pain/nausea/vomiting/constipation/new onset diabetes)
OR any age with occult blood in faeces
OR any age with rectal/abdominal mass OR unexplained anal mass/ulceration
OR ascites
OR new iron deficiency without obvious cause
OR raised/positive FIT test suggestive of cancer
OR iron deficiency anaemia with dyspepsia
OR upper GI bleeding (melaena / haematemesis)
THEN 2-Week Wait referral to gastroenterology (reflecting clinical judgement)
CONTEXT: Consider referral to MDC if raised platelets
Multiple symptom-age combinations and red flag features that mandate urgent cancer investigation via 2WW pathway. The list is exhaustive as stated in the source — each criterion is independently sufficient.
  • 🚨2WW: Refer urgently to gastroenterology (2-week wait) per NICE criteria
  • 📋Document which criterion/criteria triggered referral
  • 📋Ensure investigations are within 6 months of referral date
  • ⚠️If raised platelets: also consider MDC referral (see FBC pathway)
"2 Week Wait referral to gastroenterology: ≥40 years with jaundice or unexplained weight loss and abdominal pain; <50 years with rectal bleeding and any of the following unexplained symptoms: abdominal pain, change in bowel habit, weight loss or iron deficiency anaemia; ≥50 years with unexplained rectal bleeding; ≥60 years with iron-deficiency anaemia or changes in their bowel habit or weight loss and any of the following: diarrhoea, back pain, abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, constipation or new onset diabetes; Any age where tests show occult blood in their faeces; Any age with rectal or abdominal mass or unexplained anal mass or anal ulceration; Ascites; New iron deficiency without an obvious cause; Raised / positive FIT test suggestive of cancer; Iron deficiency anaemia with dyspepsia; Upper GI bleeding-melaena / haematemesis"

STEP 9.0 / 10.0 / 11.0 REFER EXPLICIT Refer to gastroenterology (routine/urgent) ▼
IF male AND iron deficiency anaemia AND NOT overt non-GI bleeding → refer gastro
IF postmenopausal female AND iron deficiency anaemia AND NOT overt non-GI bleeding → refer gastro
  CONTEXT: Male Hb <120 g/L OR postmeno female Hb <100 g/L → investigate MORE URGENTLY
IF age ≥50 AND (marked anaemia OR significant FH colorectal carcinoma) → refer gastro, even if coeliac found
IF premenopausal female <50 AND (colonic symptoms OR strong FH GI cancer OR persistent IDA despite treatment OR not menstruating) → refer gastro
IF coeliac serology positive → refer gastro
THEN Refer to gastroenterology (consider Advice & Guidance first if appropriate)
Men and postmenopausal women with IDA need GI investigation (cancer exclusion) unless overt non-GI bleeding explains it. Premenopausal women generally can be managed without GI investigation unless specific risk factors are present. Coeliac disease found on investigation still requires gastro involvement.
  • 🟣REFER: Gastroenterology referral (routine unless urgency criteria met)
  • 💬Consider Advice and Guidance before full referral if appropriate
  • 📋Ensure investigations within 6 months of referral date
"All men and postmenopausal women with iron deficiency anaemia unless they have overt nongastrointestinal bleeding. Men with a haemoglobin (Hb) level less than 120 g/L and postmenopausal women with an Hb level less than 100 g/L should be investigated more urgently"

STEP 5.0 / 7.0 / 7.1 / 7.2 REFER EXPLICIT Other sources of bleeding — non-GI referral routes ▼
IF other source of bleeding identified (gynaecological / epistaxis / genitourinary)
THEN Consider Advice & Guidance OR refer to relevant speciality
   Consider haematology if underlying cause unknown
   Escalate to 2WW if required (e.g. postmenopausal bleeding → gynaecology)

IF visible haematuria AND age ≥45 AND NOT UTI THEN refer urology
IF visible haematuria AND persists/recurs after UTI treatment → refer urology
IF non-visible haematuria AND age ≥60 AND (dysuria OR raised WBC) → refer urology
When bleeding source is non-GI (gynaecological, epistaxis, urinary), routing is to the relevant specialty rather than gastroenterology. Urology referral has explicit age-based thresholds for haematuria. Gynaecology 2WW applies for postmenopausal bleeding.
  • 🟣Gynaecology: 2WW for postmenopausal bleeding; routine referral otherwise
  • 🟣Urology: ≥45 with unexplained visible haematuria / ≥60 with non-visible haematuria + dysuria or raised WBC
  • 🟣Haematology: if underlying cause unknown
  • 💬Consider Advice and Guidance first where appropriate
"Consider Advice and guidance or refer to relevant speciality if underlying cause not manageable in primary care. Consider referral to haematology if underlying cause unknown. Escalate to 2WW if required e.g. Refer to gynaecology for postmenopausal bleeding. Refer to urology people aged ≥45 years with: unexplained visible haematuria without urinary tract infection or visible haematuria that persists or recurs after successful treatment of urinary tract infection. Refer to urology people aged ≥60 years with: unexplained non-visible haematuria and either dysuria or a raised white cell count on a blood test"

STEP 8.0 / 8.1 GP ACTION EXPLICIT Start iron replacement therapy ▼
IF iron deficiency anaemia suspected
THEN start iron replacement (treat whilst awaiting investigations)
1st choice: Ferrous fumarate 322mg capsule (100mg elemental iron) daily
   OR ferrous fumarate 210mg tablets (68mg elemental iron) daily
2nd choice: Ferrous sulphate 200mg (65mg elemental iron) daily
CONTEXT: Tolerance, intolerance, pregnancy, malabsorption
IF unable to tolerate oral iron → refer (usually haematology) for parenteral iron
Iron replacement should be started immediately on clinical suspicion, including while awaiting investigation results, and as a therapeutic trial in pregnant women. Diet alone is insufficient to correct IDA.
  • 💊1st line: Ferrous fumarate 322mg capsule daily (100mg elemental iron) OR ferrous fumarate 210mg tablets daily (68mg elemental iron) — take on empty stomach
  • 💊2nd line: Ferrous sulphate 200mg daily (65mg elemental iron) — take on empty stomach
  • ℹ️Consider alternate day dosing for significant GI intolerance
  • 🟣If unable to tolerate oral iron: refer to haematology for parenteral iron
  • ⚠️GI side effects (abdominal pain, constipation, black stools, nausea) — common cause of non-compliance
  • ⚠️Absorption reduced by: phytates, polyphenols (tea/coffee), calcium, antacids/PPIs
"Iron Replacement (dietary changes alone are not sufficient to correct iron deficiency anaemia). Treat whilst awaiting investigations or for therapeutic trial in pregnant women. 1st choice: Ferrous fumarate 322mg capsule (100mg elemental iron/capsule) daily or ferrous fumarate 210mg tablets (68mg elemental iron/tablet) daily. Iron should be taken on an empty stomach. 2nd choice: Ferrous sulphate 200mg (65mg elemental iron/tablet) daily."

STEP 13.0 / 14.0 / 15.0 / 18.0 MONITOR EXPLICIT FBC at 1 month — treatment response assessment ▼
IF on iron treatment
THEN check FBC in 1 month

IF significant improvement (Hb rises 1-2 g/100ml over 3-4 weeks)
THEN continue oral iron for 3 months after Hb normalisation → adequate marrow iron store repletion

IF no improvement
THEN 2WW referral OR refer to gastro/relevant specialist OR consider Advice & Guidance
Haemoglobin should rise 1-2 g/100ml over 3-4 weeks with adequate iron therapy. Failure to improve mandates further investigation — consider whether this represents occult malignancy, malabsorption, non-compliance, or incorrect diagnosis.
  • 🧪Check FBC at 1 month after starting iron
  • ✅If improved: continue iron for 3 months after Hb normalises to replete marrow stores
  • 🚨If no improvement: consider 2WW referral, refer to gastroenterology or relevant specialist
  • 💬Consider Advice and Guidance if improvement absent
"FBC in 1 month. Significant Improvement: Hb should rise by 1-2g/100ml over 3-4 weeks. Continue with oral iron for 3 months after normalisation of the Hb level, to ensure adequate repletion of the marrow iron stores."

STEP 19.0 MONITOR EXPLICIT Long-term follow-up after treatment ▼
IF Hb corrected after treatment
THEN repeat bloods 3 monthly for first year, then 6 monthly for 2-3 years
Repeat bloods again if symptoms of anaemia develop

IF iron deficiency still persisting after treatment → refer to gastroenterology
Long-term monitoring required because IDA has a tendency to recur, and the underlying cause may evolve. Regular checks allow early detection of recurrence.
  • 📅Repeat bloods every 3 months in year 1
  • 📅Then every 6 months for 2-3 years
  • 🧪Recheck if symptoms of anaemia return
  • 🟣If iron deficiency persists: refer to gastroenterology
"Recheck haemoglobin and haematinics. If iron deficiency still persisting refer to gastro. If corrected repeat bloods 3 monthly for the first year then 6 monthly for 2-3 years. Repeat bloods again if symptoms of anaemia develop."
