Additional notes from the guideline
Elevated ALT & NAFLD risk factors
The most common explanation for elevated ALT is fatty liver disease driven by diet, metabolism or alcohol; lifestyle advice should be given. Risk factors for NAFLD: obesity, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, ischaemic heart disease, family history of hypertension/IHD/type 2 diabetes, hypothyroidism, PCOS. In patients with an isolated ALT rise and these conditions it is reasonable to attribute the result to fatty liver and arrange no further investigation, unless they have an intermediate or high FIB-4 score, or features of cirrhosis on USS/examination. Persistently raised ALT without risk factors, harmful alcohol use or another cause should be considered for referral and investigation.
Harmful alcohol use
NICE definition: men >50 units/week (sustained ≥ several months); women >35 units/week (sustained); full AUDIT score >19. For alcohol dependence, the patient must be willing to consider reducing drinking. Brief intervention is effective for excessive drinking — include it when discussing the scan. Consider referral to Alcohol Services.
FIB-4, Fibroscan & ELF
FIB-4 is a liver fibrosis score from ALT, AST and platelets, with an age-modified cut-off (BSG-endorsed). Fibroscan and ELF (Enhanced Liver Fibrosis) are clinically equivalent — choice depends on local availability; ELF needs no extra blood beyond the NAFLD screen.
  • FIB-4 low (<1.3 if under 65; <2.0 if 65+): reassess risk in 3 years.
  • FIB-4 indeterminate: lab processes ELF or books Fibroscan.
  • FIB-4 high (any age >3.25): refer hepatology/gastroenterology.
  • Fibroscan ≤8 kPa or ELF <10.51: reassess in 3 years.
  • Fibroscan >8–15 kPa: consider referral, address risk factors, reassess 3–5 years.
  • Fibroscan >15 kPa or ELF ≥10.51: refer. Refer ELF ≥10.51 to secondary care (NG49).
Autoantibodies & immunoglobulins
Refer patients with abnormal LFTs and a positive liver antibody (anti-smooth-muscle actin / anti-LKM1) or strongly positive ANA (1:1600) — IgG is commonly raised in autoimmune hepatitis. Positive anti-mitochondrial antibody is strongly associated with primary biliary cholangitis (often raised IgM); if asymptomatic with normal LFTs, monitor LFTs every 2 years and refer when LFTs become abnormal or symptoms (fatigue/itch) develop. Weakly positive ANA or non-specific antibodies do not prevent Fibroscan referral if otherwise indicated.
Isolated elevated ALP & Ferritin
ALP: produced mainly by liver and bone (also gut, pancreas, kidney, placenta). When other LFTs are normal, NUH laboratory automatically quantifies GGT when ALP ≥160 U/L. Ferritin: commonest cause of a raised ferritin is fatty liver; it is also an acute-phase protein. If no acute cause, request fasting transferrin saturation on 2 occasions (raised if >55% males, >50% females). Raised ferritin <1000 with normal transferrin saturation → assume fatty liver, proceed to Fibroscan if indicated. Ferritin >1000 and/or persistently raised transferrin saturation → refer hepatology and request HFE mutation testing for haemochromatosis.
Viral hepatitis, Wilson's & Gilbert's
Hepatitis B: refer all HBsAg-positive patients; screen for HIV before referral and request HBV DNA. Hepatitis C: HCV Ab positive indicates exposure — request HCV RNA + genotype and HIV screen; refer all with HCV RNA present. Wilson's: test caeruloplasmin only if under 40 with neurological/behavioural symptoms (commonest cause of low caeruloplasmin is NAFLD); if low, send 24-hour urinary copper before referral. Gilbert's syndrome: a common cause of isolated hyperbilirubinaemia — no treatment needed beyond informing/counselling the patient.
